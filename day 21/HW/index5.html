JRR. Tolkien

>J.R.R. Tolkien (John Ronald Reuel Tolkien, 1892–1973) is widely regarded as one of the most influential authors of the 20th century, best known for his groundbreaking works of fantasy literature, most notably The Hobbit (1937) and The Lord of the Rings trilogy (1954–1955). His work has had a lasting impact on the fantasy genre, setting the foundation for modern epic fantasy and inspiring generations of writers, filmmakers, and readers.

Tolkien was born in Bloemfontein, South Africa, to English parents, but after the death of his father, the family returned to England when he was just a child. Growing up in the English countryside, Tolkien developed a love for languages, nature, and mythology. He studied at Exeter College, Oxford, where he developed a deep interest in philology (the study of languages) and ancient texts, which would profoundly influence his later writing.

Tolkien’s academic career flourished at Oxford, where he became a professor of Anglo-Saxon (Old English) at Pembroke College. His scholarly expertise in medieval literature and mythology, particularly the Beowulf epic, would play a pivotal role in shaping the mythical worlds he would later create in his fiction.
